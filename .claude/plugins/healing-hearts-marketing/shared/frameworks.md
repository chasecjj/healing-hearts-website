## Terminology Rules

These rules are mandatory across both the Jeff track and the Trisha track. When in doubt, this document wins.

### Capitalization

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| Always capitalize proprietary framework names | Critter Brain, CEO Brain, Zones of Resilience, SPARK Method, 90-Second Wave, Core Wound Map, Wound Witness, Wound Witness Covenant |
| Capitalize Zone colors when referring to the framework | Green Zone, Red Zone, Yellow Zone, Blue Zone |
| Lowercase Zone colors in general (non-framework) use | "She went into a green, calm state." |
| Capitalize "Core Wound" when using the HH concept | "His Core Wound of Rejection shapes this pattern." |
| Capitalize "Wound" when referring to a specific named wound | "the Wound of Abandonment" |
| Lowercase "core wound" in general psychological discussion | "core wounds often originate in early childhood" |
| Capitalize all 7 specific Core Wound names | Abandonment, Rejection, Unworthiness, Betrayal/Broken Trust, Neglect/Invisibility, Shame, Powerlessness |
| Italicize tool names when cross-referencing | "Return to your *Core Wound Map*..." |
| Do not capitalize attachment style names | secure, anxious, avoidant, disorganized |

| Correct | Incorrect / Avoided |
|---------|---------------------|
| activation | triggered, triggered response, triggering |

| ancient protective wiring | primitive brain, lizard brain, lower brain |

| CEO Brain | logical brain, rational mind, prefrontal cortex (in audience-facing content) |

### Avoided Terms

| Prohibited | Why |
|------------|-----|
| "primitive" (for brain) | Feels derogatory toward an adaptive response |
| "toxic" (as general modifier) | Overused; reserve for Module 2 toxic patterns context only |
| "dysfunctional" | Pathologizes; describe the pattern specifically instead |
| "broken" (about people) | Plants a label even when countered |
| "damaged" (about people) | Same as above |
| "narcissist" | Diagnosing; describe behaviors instead (manipulation, gaslighting, lack of empathy) |

### Practice vs. Exercise

| Context | Correct Term |
|---------|--------------|
| Couples doing an activity together | **practice** ("Couples Practice," "a joint practice") |
| Individual reflection/writing | **reflection prompt** |
| Individual self-rating or self-mapping | **self-assessment** |
| Never | "exercise" for any couples activity |
| Never | "journal prompt" for any individual writing |

## Framework Library

### 1. Critter Brain / CEO Brain
**Module:** 5.1 / 5.2
**One-line:** Survival brain (brainstem/limbic) vs. thinking brain (prefrontal cortex) -- the smoke alarm metaphor.
**Teaching summary:** Critter Brain is the body's built-in smoke alarm -- it fires before the thinking brain (CEO Brain) can assess whether there's a real fire. In conflict, this means you're reacting to a perceived threat, not your spouse's actual words. The entire program teaches couples to recognize when Critter Brain has taken the wheel so CEO Brain can come back online and choose a response instead of a reaction.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "You're not overreacting -- your smoke alarm is just miscalibrated. Here's what's really happening in your brain during a fight."
- "Your nervous system has a 90-second shelf life. The fight that lasted 3 hours? That was 120 hijacks in a row."

### 2. SPARK Method
**Module:** 1.4, 7.4
**One-line:** See --> Pause & Probe --> Acknowledge --> Reconnect --> Kindle.
**Teaching summary:** SPARK is a step-by-step repair process for after conflict. Instead of trying to "win" the argument or pretend it didn't happen, SPARK teaches couples to See what actually happened (separate story from sensation), Pause and Probe with curiosity, Acknowledge and validate what each person experienced, Reconnect through intentional presence, and Kindle the relationship forward. It replaces the "let's just move on" cycle with genuine repair.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "Every couple fights. The ones who last aren't the ones who fight less -- they're the ones who know how to repair. That's what SPARK teaches."
- "What if the problem isn't that you had the fight -- it's that you never learned how to come back from it?"

### 3. Zones of Resilience (Green / Yellow / Red / Blue)
**Module:** 5.4, 6.1-6.4
**One-line:** A shared nervous system language -- Green (safety/connection), Yellow (activated), Red (fight/flight), Blue (shutdown/freeze).
**Teaching summary:** The Zones of Resilience give couples a shared language for what's happening in their nervous systems so they can communicate it without blame. Green Zone is where connection, empathy, and productive conversation happen. Yellow Zone is activation -- you're starting to heat up but can still choose. Red Zone is full fight-or-flight -- CEO Brain is offline. Blue Zone is collapse, numbness, and shutdown. Knowing which zone you're in (and which zone your partner is in) changes everything about how you approach a conversation.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "You can't have a productive conversation when one of you is in Red Zone. Learning to read your zone -- and your partner's -- is the skill no one taught you."
- "Most couples try to solve problems when one or both of them are in Yellow or Red Zone. That's like trying to read a map while the car is on fire."

### 4. 90-Second Wave
**Module:** 5.3
**One-line:** Any emotion's biochemical surge lasts approximately 90 seconds -- everything after that is narrative.
**Teaching summary:** Based on neuroscientist Jill Bolte Taylor's research, the 90-Second Wave teaches that the actual biochemical flood of any emotion (rage, grief, panic) moves through your body in about 90 seconds. If you're still feeling it after that, you're re-triggering the wave with the story you're telling yourself about it. This reframe is powerful because it means you don't need to control your emotions -- you need to learn to ride the wave without adding fuel. Mastering emotions has nothing to do with controlling them.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "What if the skill isn't staying calm -- it's learning to feel the wave and let it pass?"
- "That feeling you think will last forever? Your body is done with it in 90 seconds. Your story is the part that keeps it going."

### 5. Core Wound Map (7 Wounds)
**Module:** 7.3
**One-line:** Seven foundational wounds that drive adult relationship patterns: Abandonment, Rejection, Unworthiness, Betrayal/Broken Trust, Neglect/Invisibility, Shame, Powerlessness.
**Teaching summary:** The Core Wound Map helps individuals identify which childhood wounds are running their adult relationship patterns. Most people carry one or two dominant wounds that get activated by their partner without either person understanding why. When your spouse forgets to call, your Wound of Abandonment might fire -- and suddenly you're not reacting to a missed call, you're reacting to every time you were left as a child. The map traces the chain: Wound --> Belief --> Pattern --> Trigger, giving couples a way to see what's really driving their conflicts.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "You're not reacting to what your spouse just said. You're reacting to a wound they didn't create."
- "The fight about the dishes was never about the dishes. It was about the Wound of Invisibility -- feeling like you don't matter enough to be noticed."

### 6. Wound Witness / Wound Witness Covenant
**Module:** 7.3
**One-line:** A sacred disclosure practice where partners share their Core Wounds, protected by a covenant that what is shared will never be weaponized.
**Teaching summary:** The Wound Witness practice is one of the most vulnerable activities in the program. After each partner has mapped their Core Wounds, they share them with each other in a structured, regulated environment. Before beginning, both partners agree to the Wound Witness Covenant: what is shared in this practice is sacred and will never be used as ammunition in a future argument. This practice builds deep intimacy because it replaces judgment with understanding -- when you know your partner's wound, you stop taking their reactions personally and start seeing the scared kid behind the defensive adult.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "What if the bravest thing you could do for your marriage wasn't fixing the problem -- but showing your partner the wound underneath it?"
- "Real intimacy isn't just knowing your partner's favorite movie. It's knowing their deepest wound and choosing to protect it instead of poke it."

### 7. Invisible Backpack
**Module:** Coaching (woven throughout curriculum)
**One-line:** Childhood protection strategies carried into adult relationships -- the coping tools that kept you safe as a kid but create conflict in your marriage.
**Teaching summary:** Everyone carries an Invisible Backpack full of protection strategies they learned in childhood. The kid who learned "don't show weakness" becomes the physician who shuts down when their spouse needs vulnerability. The kid who learned "be perfect enough and they'll stay" becomes the spouse who takes every critique as abandonment. These strategies made perfect sense when you were small -- they were survival tools. But in your marriage, they create the exact patterns you're trying to escape. The Invisible Backpack framework helps couples stop blaming each other and start seeing the real source of their reactive patterns.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "You're not reacting to your spouse -- you're reacting to a wound they didn't create. That reaction? It's been in your backpack since childhood."
- "The physician who 'can't open up' isn't being stubborn. They learned as a kid that showing weakness was dangerous. That backpack is heavy -- and invisible."

### 8. 6 Levels of Intimacy
**Module:** 1.3, 7.4
**One-line:** Trust --> Honesty --> Commitment --> Verbal/Emotional/Intellectual Connection --> Spiritual --> Physical/Sexual. Intimacy is built as a progression, not a destination.
**Teaching summary:** The 6 Levels of Intimacy framework teaches that physical and sexual intimacy is the top of a building, not the foundation. Most couples try to fix their physical connection without realizing the floors below it are cracked. Trust comes first, then honesty, then commitment, then verbal/emotional/intellectual connection, then spiritual intimacy, and only then does physical/sexual intimacy have a stable foundation. When couples understand this progression, they stop fighting about the symptom (physical disconnection) and start rebuilding from the actual point of fracture.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "If you're fighting about intimacy, the real problem is probably three floors below it. Here's where to actually start."
- "Physical disconnection is almost never about physical attraction. It's about which level of intimacy broke first."

### 9. Hartman Color Code (Red / Blue / White / Yellow)
**Module:** 1.2
**One-line:** Motive-based personality wiring -- Red (power), Blue (intimacy), White (peace), Yellow (fun). Lenses, not flaws.
**Teaching summary:** The Hartman Color Code helps couples understand that their partner's behavior comes from a fundamentally different motivational wiring -- not a character flaw. Reds are driven by power and results, Blues by intimacy and connection, Whites by peace and autonomy, Yellows by fun and spontaneity. When a Red spouse takes charge of vacation planning and a White spouse withdraws, neither is being difficult -- they're operating from their core motive. The framework stops the cycle of trying to change each other's nature and starts the conversation about how different wiring can complement rather than collide.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "You've been trying to change your partner's personality for years. What if the answer isn't changing them -- it's understanding what drives them?"
- "She needs connection. He needs peace. Neither is wrong -- they're just speaking different motivational languages."

### 10. The Coping Ladder (Restorative / Escape / Numbing)
**Module:** 8.2
**One-line:** A non-judgmental self-assessment of coping strategies arranged in three tiers: Restorative (building capacity), Escape (temporary relief), Numbing (shutting down sensation).
**Teaching summary:** The Coping Ladder gives individuals an honest, shame-free way to evaluate how they're dealing with stress. Restorative strategies (movement, genuine connection, creative expression, nature, Sabbath rest) actually rebuild capacity. Escape strategies (binge-watching, retail therapy, social media) provide temporary relief but don't restore anything. Numbing strategies (alcohol, overwork, rage, pornography, doomscrolling) actively suppress sensation and create their own damage. The framework is explicitly non-judgmental -- the goal isn't shame, it's awareness. Most people use all three tiers; the question is which tier you default to under pressure.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "There's a difference between coping and numbing. One rebuilds you. The other just turns the volume down until it doesn't."
- "Overwork is the numbing strategy most rewarded by our culture. That doesn't make it healthy."

### 11. Hidden Blueprints
**Module:** 7.1, F.1
**One-line:** Childhood-programmed beliefs that silently drive adult behavior in relationships, finances, parenting, and career.
**Teaching summary:** Hidden Blueprints are the unconscious rulebooks written in childhood -- before age 8, absorbed without context or consent. They govern what you believe about money ("rich people are selfish"), about conflict ("if I speak up, I'll be punished"), about love ("I have to earn it"), and about yourself ("I'm not enough"). These blueprints run silently in the background, and what looks like self-sabotage is actually faithful execution of an outdated program. The framework teaches couples to surface, name, and consciously rewrite these blueprints rather than being run by them.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "You're not self-sabotaging. You're following a blueprint you didn't write, from a life you no longer live."
- "Your money fights aren't about money. They're about the Hidden Blueprint each of you absorbed before age 8."

### 12. 13 Principles of the Subconscious
**Module:** 7.1 (adapted from Thais Gibson)
**One-line:** The operating rules of the subconscious mind -- it runs 95-97% of processing, speaks emotion and imagery (not logic), prefers familiarity over happiness, and has no "off switch."
**Teaching summary:** The 13 Principles of the Subconscious explain why knowing something logically doesn't change the behavior. Your subconscious doesn't respond to rational arguments -- it responds to emotion, repetition, and vivid imagery. It will choose a familiar misery over an unfamiliar joy because familiarity equals safety. It doesn't process negatives ("don't shut down" registers as "SHUT DOWN"). Understanding these principles is the prerequisite for all the reprogramming work in the course -- you can't rewrite the software if you don't understand the operating system. The Pink Elephant Principle alone changes how couples communicate: "Stop shutting down" becomes "Stay here with me."
**Marketing hooks:**
- "Your brain will choose a familiar misery over an unfamiliar joy. That's not weakness -- that's your subconscious doing its job. Here's how to update the programming."
- "'Don't shut down' is actually a command TO shut down. Your subconscious doesn't hear the 'don't.' Here's what to say instead."

### 13. The Sacred Exchange
**Module:** 7.2
**One-line:** A Christ-centered forgiveness framework that separates forgiveness from trust and reconciliation.
**Teaching summary:** The Sacred Exchange teaches that forgiveness, trust, and reconciliation are three distinct things -- and confusing them keeps people trapped. You can forgive someone without trusting them again. You can rebuild trust without full reconciliation. This framework is grounded in a faith perspective, framing the Atonement as an "embodied exchange" -- the weight of resentment is real, stored in the body, and releasing it is both a spiritual and a physiological act. Unforgiveness keeps the nervous system in survival mode and shrinks the Green Zone. The Sacred Exchange is positioned as faith-woven-in, never faith-weaponized -- available for those who want it, never imposed.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "Forgiveness and trust are not the same thing. Confusing them is why so many people stay stuck."
- "Resentment isn't just an emotional weight -- it's a physiological one. Your nervous system is paying the bill for every grudge you're carrying."

### 14. Fix-It Embargo
**Module:** 8.1
**One-line:** Listen first, don't solve -- a burnout recovery tool that breaks the physician habit of diagnosing your spouse's feelings.
**Teaching summary:** The Fix-It Embargo is specifically designed for couples where one partner (often the physician) defaults to problem-solving when their spouse shares a struggle. The physician brain is trained to hear a symptom and prescribe a solution -- but in a marriage, that lands as dismissal. "Have you tried..." is the five words that make a spouse feel most unseen. The Fix-It Embargo is a structured practice: when your partner shares, your only job is to listen and validate. No solutions. No reframes. No silver linings. Just "that sounds really hard" and presence. It's one of the simplest tools in the program and one of the most immediately transformative.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "The five words that are quietly destroying physician marriages: 'Have you tried...' Here's what to say instead."
- "You were trained to diagnose and solve. Your spouse doesn't need a doctor right now. They need a witness."

### 15. Financial Nervous System
**Module:** F.1
**One-line:** Money triggers mapped to fight/flight/freeze -- couples rarely fight about math; they fight about what money means.
**Teaching summary:** The Financial Nervous System framework applies the same nervous system science from the relationship modules to money conflicts. When couples fight about spending, saving, or debt, the fight is rarely about the numbers -- it's about the meaning each person's nervous system has attached to money based on their childhood Hidden Blueprints. One partner's "we need to save more" activates the other's Wound of Powerlessness. One partner's spontaneous purchase activates the other's Critter Brain because money scarcity was a childhood threat. The framework teaches couples to regulate their financial nervous system before making joint decisions, using the same Zone awareness they've already learned: "Are we both in Green Zone right now, or is one of us activated?"
**Marketing hooks:**
- "Couples rarely fight about math. They fight about what money meant in the house they grew up in."
- "A budget isn't a prison -- it's a flashlight. And you can't hold a flashlight steady when your nervous system is in Red Zone."

## Cross-Framework Connections

Several frameworks reinforce each other and can be paired in content:

| Pair | Connection |
|------|------------|
| Critter Brain + 90-Second Wave | The hijack fires; the wave lasts 90 seconds. Learning both means you can name what's happening AND know it will pass. |
| Core Wound Map + Invisible Backpack | The backpack contains the wounds. The map names them. Together they answer "why do I react like this?" |
| Hidden Blueprints + Financial Nervous System | Financial blueprints are the money-specific subset of Hidden Blueprints. Same mechanism, different domain. |
| Zones of Resilience + SPARK Method | You need to be in Green Zone (or at least Yellow) to run SPARK. Zone awareness is the prerequisite; SPARK is the repair tool. |
| Fix-It Embargo + 6 Levels of Intimacy | The Embargo rebuilds the lower floors (trust, honesty, emotional connection) that Fix-It mode erodes. |
| Coping Ladder + Critter Brain | Numbing strategies are what Critter Brain reaches for when it wants the activation to stop. The Ladder makes the pattern visible. |
| Attachment Styles + Core Wound Map | Your attachment style is the armor you built around your core wounds. The Octopus pursues because abandonment is unbearable. The Turtle withdraws because rejection is unbearable. |

### Attachment Styles (Octopus / Turtle / Safe Harbor)
**Module:** 4.1-4.4
**One-line:** Three attachment patterns — Octopus (anxious/pursuer), Turtle (avoidant/withdrawer), Safe Harbor (secure) — that explain why couples get stuck in the same cycle.
**Teaching summary:** Most couples live in the Octopus-Turtle cycle: one partner pursues connection harder while the other withdraws further. Both are terrified — the Octopus fears abandonment, the Turtle fears engulfment. Neither is wrong; they're both running childhood protection strategies. Understanding your attachment style stops the blame game and starts the repair process. Each style has a childhood origin, a relational pattern, a conflict style, and core beliefs that drive it.
**Marketing hooks:**
- "You're not needy. You're not cold. You're an Octopus married to a Turtle — and neither of you knows the other one is scared."
- "Take the attachment style quiz and finally understand why your partner does THAT thing."
