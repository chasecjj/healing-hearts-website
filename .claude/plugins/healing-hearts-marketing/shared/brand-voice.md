# Healing Hearts Brand Voice

The neutral HH voice — used when content is not attributed to Jeff or Trisha personally. This is the voice of Healing Hearts as an entity: warm, grounded, hope-forward, and deeply specific to the medical marriage experience.

---

## When to Use

- **Website copy** — headlines, section text, pillar descriptions, CTAs, pull quotes (anything not inside a Jeff or Trisha attributed block)
- **Emails from "The Healing Hearts Team"** — welcome sequences, nurture emails, transactional emails signed "With hope, The Healing Hearts Team"
- **Social posts from brand accounts** — Instagram carousels, Facebook posts, or any content posted as Healing Hearts (not Jeff's personal LinkedIn or Trisha's personal accounts)
- **Marketing materials** — booth signage, expo banners, print ads, QR code landing pages, brochures
- **Course portal UI copy** — module descriptions, lesson intros, progress encouragement, onboarding flows
- **Podcast show notes and episode descriptions** — the framing around Jeff and Trisha's conversations

Do NOT use the brand voice for Jeff-attributed LinkedIn posts, Trisha-voiced curriculum content, or any piece where the reader expects to hear from a specific person.

---

## Voice Characteristics

The brand voice sits in the space between Jeff and Trisha — it carries their shared conviction without either person's individual mannerisms. It sounds like a couple who has been through it, studied it, and now holds the door open for others.

**Core qualities:**

- **Warm, not performative.** Warmth comes from specificity and honesty, not from exclamation points or superlatives. The reader should feel understood, not cheered at.
- **Hope-forward, not naive.** Every piece acknowledges how hard this is before pointing toward what's possible. Hope is earned by naming the pain first.
- **Never clinical or cold.** The science is present but wears everyday clothes. "Your nervous system fires before your thinking brain can catch up" — not "amygdala-mediated threat response precedes prefrontal cortex engagement."
- **"You're not broken — your pattern is."** This is the brand's north star reframe. The problem is never the person. It's the protection strategy that outlived its usefulness.
- **Speaks to the couple, not at them.** Uses "you" and "we" fluidly. Never lectures. The posture is: we've been where you are, and we found a way through.
- **Never shame-based or fear-driven.** The brand never tells anyone their marriage is failing. It says: your pattern is the problem, and patterns can change.
- **Professional but accessible.** Not academic. Not sloppy. The tone of a trusted friend who happens to know the research — and introduces it through insight, not credentials.
- **Distinctly HH.** Every piece should feel like only Healing Hearts could have written it. If a generic relationship brand could publish it, it's not specific enough.

## Tone Rules

### Sentence Rhythm
Short declarative sentences carry key truths. Longer sentences carry explanation and nuance. The rhythm alternates: short punch, longer elaboration, short punch. Reading it aloud should feel natural, not metered.

**Example from the website:**
> "Your marriage is not broken. It's buried. Underneath everything you were never taught."

Three sentences. The first is a direct refutation. The second is two words. The third opens the door to curiosity.

### Opening Pattern
Lead with a direct refutation of a common belief, or name the reader's experience more accurately than they've named it themselves. Never open with a generic setup.

**Example from the website:**
> "You've tried everything. Nothing is sticking. You've read the books. Maybe you've sat in a therapist's office and nodded along while the clock ran out. You've had the tearful conversations at midnight that felt like breakthroughs but changed nothing by Tuesday morning."

This works because it describes the reader's life with enough specificity that they feel seen.

### Metaphor Usage
Every abstract concept gets at least one concrete, everyday metaphor. The metaphors are domestic and embodied — smoke alarms, invisible backpacks, Coca-Cola bottles, trees. They are never forced or decorative. They exist to make a concept stick.

**Example from the email sequence:**
> "Something your spouse said brushed against a wound you've been carrying since long before you met them. And your nervous system — brilliant, protective, and completely unhelpful in this moment — launched a defense strategy you learned as a kid."

The nervous system is described in three adjectives that humanize it. The "defense strategy you learned as a kid" makes an abstract neurological process tangible.

### "We" Language
The brand uses "we" in two ways: (1) "we" as Healing Hearts the team ("we teach," "we believe"), and (2) "we" as shared human experience ("we all carry invisible backpacks"). Both are appropriate. The second is more powerful and should be used whenever the brand is normalizing a struggle.

### Faith Integration
Faith is woven in, not bolted on. References to marriage as sacred, to hope, to covenant are natural and present. Specific theological claims, scripture citations, or denominational language are not used. The tone is: "If your faith is part of how you understand your relationship, it's welcome here."

**Example from the website:**
> "We believe marriages are sacred. Our faith shapes how we see covenant and forgiveness — but we never weaponize scripture."

> "We believe God designed the nervous system, wrote attachment into our DNA, and invites us into the honest, sometimes messy work of becoming safe for the people we love."

## What This Voice Is NOT

- **NOT Trisha's voice.** Trisha's voice is warmer, more story-driven, and grounded in personal anecdotes with Jeff. She uses phrases like "this makes sense" and "that's huge." She shares vulnerable moments from her own marriage. The brand voice does not tell personal stories.
- **NOT Jeff's voice.** Jeff's voice is Socratic — he leads with questions, uses "I see this in my practice," references his clinical experience, and builds to an insight rather than stating conclusions. The brand voice does not ask rhetorical questions as a primary teaching tool or invoke clinical authority.
- **The brand voice sits between them** — it carries their shared warmth without Trisha's personal vulnerability or Jeff's Socratic questioning. It is the couple's conviction distilled into a single, neutral presence.
- **NOT a committee voice.** The brand voice should never sound like it was written by a marketing team. It should sound like it was written by people who have been through something real and came out the other side with tools to share.

## Anti-Patterns

### Never Use

- **Generic motivational content.** "Communication is key to a healthy marriage! Here are 5 tips..." could be any relationship brand. Every piece must teach or reference a specific HH framework (Critter Brain, SPARK, Attachment Styles, Invisible Backpack, Color Code).
- **Fear-based content.** "Your marriage is failing and here's why." Healing Hearts is hope-forward. The message is always: your pattern is the problem, and patterns can change.
- **Clinical or therapeutic language.** "This exercise will heal your attachment trauma." HH is educational, not therapeutic. Use "explore," "understand," "recognize" — never "heal," "treat," or "diagnose" as promises.
- **Content a competitor could publish.** If a generic relationship coach could put their name on it, it's not specific enough to HH. Framework anchoring is what separates this brand.
- **Toxic positivity or false uplift.** "You have everything you need to heal your relationship!" with exclamation points and performed enthusiasm. The brand never overclaims. It earns trust by being honest about how hard the work is.

### Specific Phrases to Avoid

| Avoid | Why |
|-------|-----|
| "In today's fast-paced world..." | Generic AI opener. Never use as an introduction. |
| "healing journey" | Wellness-blog cliche. Describe what the healing actually involves. |
| "communication issues" | Vague. Name the specific pattern instead. |
| "triggered" | Overused in pop psychology. Use "activated" or "activation." |
| "dysfunction" | Pathologizing. Describe the pattern instead. |
| "it's important to note that" | Unnecessary throat-clearing. State the thing directly. |
| "Additionally" / "Furthermore" | Academic transition words. Use narrative connectors. |
| "strategies" (standalone) | Sounds like a business manual. Use "tools," "practices," or name the specific thing. |
| Exclamation-point enthusiasm | The brand's warmth comes from specificity and honesty, not punctuation. One exclamation point per 500 words maximum — and only in genuinely celebratory moments. |

### Tone Drift Warnings

- **If it sounds like a textbook:** You've drifted clinical. Ground every concept in an everyday metaphor.
- **If it sounds like a wellness blog:** You've gone generic. Anchor to a specific HH framework.
- **If it sounds like a sales page:** You've drifted promotional. Teach first, sell second. The CTA should feel like a natural next step, not a pitch.
- **If it could be published by any relationship brand:** It's not specific enough. Add an HH framework reference or a medical-marriage-specific detail.

## Gold Standard Examples

### Example 1: Website Hero Copy (Brand Voice at Its Most Distilled)

> "Your marriage is not broken. It's buried. Underneath everything you were never taught. We help you understand why you shut down, and how to come back to each other."

**What makes it work:** Four sentences. The first refutes the reader's worst fear. The second reframes it in two words. The third names the real problem (missing education, not missing love). The fourth positions HH as guide, not savior. No exclamation points. No promises. Just clarity.

### Example 2: Website Section — Naming the Reader's Experience

> "We go beneath the arguments. Past the surface. Into the nervous system, the attachment wounds, and the invisible scripts your childhood wrote."

**What makes it work:** Short fragments build momentum. Each one goes deeper — arguments, then surface, then nervous system, then childhood. The phrase "invisible scripts your childhood wrote" is specific to HH's framework worldview. A generic brand would say "communication patterns."

### Example 3: Email Body — Teaching One Concept Through a Relatable Moment

> "You know that fight? The one about the dishes. Or the thermostat. Or whose turn it is to pick up the kids. It's never about the dishes. What's actually happening: something your spouse said or did brushed against a wound you've been carrying since long before you met them. Neither of you is wrong. You're just carrying backpacks the other person can't see."

**What makes it work:** Opens with the most universal couple experience (the dishes fight). Delivers the reframe plainly ("It's never about the dishes"). Names the Invisible Backpack framework without jargon. Ends with a sentence that removes blame from both partners simultaneously. Signed "With hope, The Healing Hearts Team" — not attributed to Jeff or Trisha individually.

### Example 4: Website Belief Statement — Faith Woven In

> "Our work is Christ-centered, but it's never simplistic. We don't believe 'just pray harder' is a relationship strategy. We believe God designed the nervous system, wrote attachment into our DNA, and invites us into the honest, sometimes messy work of becoming safe for the people we love."

**What makes it work:** Addresses the faith integration directly and honestly. Refutes the simplistic version first ("just pray harder"). Then states the actual belief in a way that integrates science and faith without tension. "Sometimes messy" is the kind of honest qualifier that builds trust.
